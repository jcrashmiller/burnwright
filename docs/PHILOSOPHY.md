# The burnwright philosophy

## You bought it. It's yours.

There was a time when buying something meant owning it. You handed over money, they handed over a thing, and that was the end of the transaction. What you did with the thing afterward was your business. You could use it, lend it, modify it, preserve it, or set it on fire in your backyard — none of which required the seller's ongoing permission, a subscription, an internet connection, or a terms-of-service agreement.

That relationship has been quietly dismantled over the past thirty years, and the dismantling accelerated with digital media. The mechanism was licensing. Instead of selling you a movie, a studio licenses you the right to watch it under conditions they can revise at any time. Instead of selling you a game, a publisher sells you a key to a server they control. When the server goes down, or the company folds, or they decide your behavior violates their terms, your "purchase" evaporates.

Ubisoft's CEO said the quiet part out loud: customers need to get comfortable with not owning their games. The direction of travel is clear. Ownership is being replaced by access, and access can always be revoked.

burnwright is a small act of refusal.

## What physical media actually means

A pressed CD-R with data encoded on it is yours in a way that a streaming license is not. The ones and zeros are physically present on the disc. They do not phone home. They do not require an account. They do not stop working when a company goes bankrupt or a licensing deal expires. You can put the disc in a drawer for twenty years and it will play the same content it played the day you burned it.

This is not nostalgia for its own sake. It is a meaningful technical and philosophical distinction. Data you physically possess cannot be remotely revoked. A disc you burned yourself is yours in the same sense that a book on your shelf is yours.

VCD and SVCD are not the highest-quality formats available. That is not the point. The point is that a VCD is a self-contained artifact. Everything needed to play it is on the disc. No server. No account. No permission required.

## The screener experience

There is something else about VCD and SVCD that is worth saying.

When you watch a movie on a DVD, you navigate a menu. You select your language. You sit through anti-piracy warnings. You might be offered a choice of aspect ratio. The studio has inserted itself between you and the film.

When you watch a movie at a cinema, none of that happens. The lights go down. The film starts. There is no menu, no commentary track, no special features. Just the film, running from beginning to end. When the reel changed, the projectionist made it happen and you barely noticed. You were watching the story.

A VCD screener — a disc with no menu, no PBC, no extras, just the video stream — is the closest home viewing gets to that theatrical experience. You put the disc in and the movie starts. When disc one ends, you put in disc two and it continues. The interruption is not a technical failure. It is a natural pause, a moment to stretch and get more popcorn, not unlike intermission in the era when films were long enough to require one.

burnwright produces discs that work this way by default. No menus unless you specifically ask for them. No interruptions. The disc goes in and the content plays.

## UserEnd

The tools in this collection are built on a principle we call UserEnd.

The conventional term is "end user" — a phrase that positions you as the terminal point of a supply chain. The end user receives the product. The end user accepts the terms. The end user is the last link in a chain that begins with the developer and ends with you.

UserEnd inverts this. The relationship ends with the user. Once the software leaves our hands and reaches yours, our involvement is over. We do not need to know what you do with it. We do not want ongoing access to your system. We are not interested in your usage data. We will not send you notifications, request reviews, or ask you to create an account.

You have the tool. The tool does the thing. Our relationship is complete.

This is how a hammer works. When a carpenter buys a hammer, the manufacturer does not retain the right to audit what the carpenter builds, update the hammer's firmware, or revoke access if the carpenter uses it on an unapproved nail. The transaction is final. The object belongs to its new owner entirely.

burnwright is a hammer. Use it however you need to. We will never know, and that is exactly as it should be.

## On digital sovereignty

Several European governments — France, Denmark, Germany, and others — have begun actively moving their public institutions away from proprietary American software toward open-source alternatives. They use the phrase "digital sovereignty": the right of individuals, organizations, and nations to control their own digital infrastructure rather than depending on the goodwill of foreign corporations.

burnwright is a small personal expression of the same idea. You should not need a corporation's permission to watch a film you paid for. You should not need a streaming service's servers to be running in order to watch something you own. You should be able to preserve your media in a format you control, on physical media you possess, playable on hardware you own, without asking anyone's permission.

That is digital sovereignty at the individual level. It is not complicated. It just requires tools that respect your ownership instead of undermining it.

burnwright tries to be one of those tools.
